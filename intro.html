Introduction to Drought in India

Drought is a recurring and devastating natural phenomenon that has significant consequences for agriculture, water resources, and ecosystems. In India, a country that depends heavily on agriculture for both its economy and food security, droughts are among the most common natural disasters, and their impacts are particularly severe.

Impact of the Monsoon

A drought is typically characterized by prolonged periods of low rainfall, leading to water shortages that affect agriculture, industry, and human consumption. In India, the consequences of drought can be far-reaching, not only causing food and water scarcity but also triggering social and economic crises. India’s vulnerability to droughts has increased in recent decades, largely due to changing climatic patterns, overexploitation of groundwater, and environmental degradation.

Causes of Drought

One of the key factors contributing to droughts in India is the monsoon season, which traditionally brings the majority of the country’s rainfall. The Indian Monsoon, however, is increasingly becoming erratic due to changing weather patterns and climate change. India typically receives rainfall during two monsoon phases: the southwest monsoon (June to September) and the northeast monsoon (October to December).

Human Activities and Drought

In addition to the natural variability in rainfall, human activities have also worsened the impact of droughts in India. Over-extraction of groundwater for agricultural irrigation, unchecked urbanization, and deforestation have compounded the country’s vulnerability to water scarcity. The over-extraction of groundwater, in particular, has left many aquifers depleted, making it difficult for regions to recover from droughts.

Effects on Agriculture and Society

Agriculture in India is particularly susceptible to drought due to its dependence on seasonal rains for irrigation. Around 60-70% of Indian agriculture is rainfed, making it extremely vulnerable to changes in monsoon patterns. As drought conditions persist, the country faces crop failures and food shortages. The inability of farmers to recover from repeated droughts has also led to a rise in farmer suicides, further highlighting the socio-economic toll of these environmental disasters.

Water Scarcity and Urban Crises

The consequences of droughts in India are not just confined to agriculture. As water resources dwindle, both rural and urban populations are left with limited access to drinking water. In some regions, people have to rely on tankers for water supply, and there are reports of water scarcity in cities like Chennai and Delhi.

Environmental and Ecological Impact

In addition to water and food insecurity, droughts in India also have ecological consequences. Prolonged drought leads to soil degradation, which reduces agricultural productivity and worsens food insecurity. The loss of vegetation due to water shortages results in increased desertification and reduced biodiversity.

Government Response and Measures

India’s response to droughts has included both short-term and long-term measures. The government often implements emergency relief measures such as distributing drinking water, providing fodder for livestock, and offering financial aid to farmers. In some cases, the government also attempts to mitigate the effects of drought by providing subsidies for rainwater harvesting, promoting water-efficient irrigation technologies like drip irrigation, and encouraging the restoration of degraded lands.
